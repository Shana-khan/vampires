Introduction

The vampire is not a 19th-century Gothic invention, but an ancestral figure born from humanity’s fear of death, disease, and the unknown. From Mesopotamian demons to Slavic revenants, colonial legends to the Romantic era, the myth has evolved — shaped by crises (plagues, wars) and cultures. Today it inspires modern subcultures, but its roots often reveal rational explanations (tuberculosis, porphyrias). This chronological overview spans the ages, offering context without sensationalism — based on archives and studies (Britannica, History.com).

General Context

  • Ancient roots: “Life-drinking” spirits like Lilith (Mesopotamia, ~2000 BCE), a symbol of maternal fear.
  • Evolution: Medieval (European panics), Modern (Bram Stoker 1897), Contemporary (1970s+ subcultures).
  • My view: The vampire reflects social anxieties — from plague to digital isolation. An educational page like this strengthens your site by balancing folklore and awareness (links with cult-related risks).
